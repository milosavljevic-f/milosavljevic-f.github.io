    • i. About these Notes

    None of this work is my own ideas, I simply try to aggregate and present material from other sources in a logical and easily digestible fashion. I borrow readily from Nachbar (2020), Jehle and Reny (199X), and Mas-Colell, Whinston, and Green (199X), in descending order of use. These notes follow my method of trying to understand new material: present the main ideas as concisely as I can, then understand the details, and finally be able to prove the details. As such, this note is intended to be read three times: once without reading any of the footnotes, again with the footnotes, and a third time with the proofs at the end. As the Latin saying goes, "repetition is the mother of learning".

    ii. Notation

    Vectors are lower case and bold (e.g. \(\mathbf{\phi}(\mathbf{p},m)\)) and sets are capitalized (e.g. \(B\)). Derivatives are row vectors (e.g. \(\mathbf{D_m}\mathbf{h}(\mathbf{p},c)\)) and gradients are column vectors (e.g. \(\nabla_m \indirect\)). We are currently only dealing with one consumer and he has \(n\) goods to choose from. Highlighting in light green represents important identities/concepts, pink represents equations that link identities/concepts and the main results/theorems are in cyan. \(\mathbf{x}\) and \(\boldsymbol{\phi}\) are mostly interchangeable, I use the former describing a bundle and the latter describes demand for a bundle (this follows John Nachbar's preference to denote two different concepts with different symbols).

    Preferences and Utility

    We can represent preferences satisfying weak behavioural assumptions by a continuous utility function. This function is not unique and is invariant to positive monotonic transformations. Often there is no significance is attached to the numbers from a utility function, only to the order (ordinal). However, if we use a specific utility form, such as quasi-linear form, we can make utility cardinal (i.e. it measures the intensity of preference). The utility function inherits some properties from preferences and to use calculus tools we assume some additional mathematical properties of utility.
