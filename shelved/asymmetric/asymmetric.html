Introduction

We studied the two fundamental welfare theorems. A key assumption to the first welfare theorem is there are complete markets, and for there to be complete markets, the characteristics of the market must be observable by all agents, which is absent in asymmetrical information.

Asymmetric information is usually distinguished by two types: adverse selection and moral hazard. In the former there is asymmetric information at the time of contracting and in the latter one party cannot perfectly observe what the other does after the contract is made. There are two important mechanisms that emerge in the market to deal with adverse selection: signaling and screening. The structure of the notes is as follows: [[flow.png]]

Moral hazard (hidden action)

Principal-agent model

Moral hazard models have hidden action1 and, as a result, asymmetric information forms. Typically, this takes the form of a principal-agent problem, where the agent has more information about their actions (typically exerting costly effort2) and the principal cannot completely monitor them. The effort of the agent generates some random outcome3 of the agent’s effort x ∈ [xL, xH] with a c.d.f. F( ⋅ |e).

The problem is that principal wishes to hire the agent and must decide what what contract to set, such that the principal’s utility x − w(x) is maximized (for simplicity the principal is risk-neutral4). Thus the optimal contract is a mapping from the outcome x to a wage payment: w : x → ℝ.

If actions were observable, the optimal contract problem would be straightforward; the contract would specify the actions taken by the agent and the compensation provided in return. The principal would be able to achieve the ‘first-best’ contract. In the next section we study this case first. However, we can also implement the first-best contract when the agent is [[Asymmetric Information#Incomplete information and a risk-neutral agent|risk-neutral]]. When we consider a [[Asymmetric Information#Incomplete information and a risk-averse agent|risk-averse]] agent, or another type of friction (such as [[Asymmetric Information#Limited liability problem|limited liability]]), we lose the ability to implement the first best.

Complete information about agent’s effort

Note that we do not require a condition for the agent to exert high effort since the agent can be forced to exert high effort. If the agent were not choosing high effort, his action could be perfectly detected by the principal, and hence the agent could be heavily punished. However, we do require a condition that says the agent will receive greater utility from doing the action than his outside option (e.g. not accepting the contract at all). This is known as the individual rationality (IR) constraint. The optimal contract for the principal solves the following problem:5
maxw(x)∫xLxH(x − w(x))f(x|e)dx

$$\text{s.t.} \int_{x_L}^{x_H} v \big(w(x) \big)f(x|e)dx - g(e) \geq \overline{u}$$
where v( ⋅ ) is the agent’s utility and g(e) is the disutility from effort.

The first order condition for this problem gives:
$$\frac{1}{v'(w(x))}=\lambda$$
Which implies that the optimal compensation scheme w(x) is a constant (it doesn’t depend on x). Also, we know that the IR constraint always binds at a solution to this problem; otherwise, the owner could lower the agent’s wages while still getting him to accept the contract. Using this fact we can solve for the wage: $w(x)= v^{-1} \Big( \overline{u}+g(e) \Big)$.

We should also provide the equation6 that specifies the optimal choice of e:
$$e^* = \text{argmax}_e \int x f(x|e)dx - v^{-1} \big( \overline{u} + g(e) \big)$$

This optimal contract achieves two goals: it gives the efficient choice by the agent (e*), and insures the agent against income risk (the agent will always get the constant w).

Incomplete Information about agent’s effort

Incomplete information and a risk-neutral agent

It is not always necessary for there to be complete information for the first-best contract to be implementable. In this section we consider the case of a risk-neutral agent, however, this will also be achievable if the optimal level of effort is also the least costly level of effort (e.g. high effort is no more costly than low effort), or in the case of a ‘shifting-support’ (that is, ∃S s.t. ℙ(x ∈ S|e = e*)).7 Since the agent’s effort is now not observable, we have a new constraint; the constraint that the agent must actually want to choose a given level of effort (we will restrict our attention to the case when the principal is trying to induce high effort) when facing the incentive scheme. This is known as the incentive compatibility (IC) constraint and is the third condition in the agent’s new problem:8
maxw(x)∫(x − w(x))f(x|e)dx

$$\text{s.t.} \int w(x)f(x|e)dx - g(e) \geq \overline{u}$$

∫w(x)f(x|eH)dx − g(eH) ≥ ∫w(x)f(x|eL)dx − g(eL)

The solution to this problem is that the owner can offer the compensation scheme w(x) = x − α. This is often interpreted as “selling the project to the agent” because the agent receives the full return x except a constant payment α (the “sale price”). If the agent accepts this contract they will choose a level of effort to maximize utility:
maxe∫w(x)f(x|e)dx − g(e) = maxe∫xf(x|e)dx − α − g(e)
However, notice that this will be maximized at the same e* as the complete information effort is maximized at. Thus, this contract induces the first-best effort e*. The agent will accept the contract as long as the IR is satisfied:
$$\int x f(x|e^*)dx - \alpha - g(e^*) \geq \overline{u}$$
Since we know that the principal can increase his utility by increasing α, he will increase it to the point where the above IR binds. Thus, we can solve for α and hence our optimal compensation scheme w(x).9 The intuition behind being able to achieve the first-best contract in this case is because there is no trade-off between inducing effort and sharing risk. However, as we will see in the following section, if the agent is risk-averse then incentivizing the agent to induce high effort comes with risk that the agent does not like to take.

Incomplete information and a risk-averse agent

The problem here is rather similar to the previous one, except we cannot make the simplification v(x) = x:
maxw(x)∫(x − w(x))f(x|e)dx

$$\text{s.t.} \int v \big( w(x) \big) f(x|e) dx - g(e) \geq \overline{u}$$

∫v(w(x))f(x|eH)dx − g(eH) ≥ ∫v(w(x))f(x|eL)dx − g(eL)
The first order conditions of this problem (with λ and μ the Lagrange multipliers on the IR and IC, respectively) are:
$$\frac{1}{v'(w(x))}=\lambda + \mu \bigg[ 1 - \frac{f(x|e_L)}{f(x|e_H)} \bigg]$$

To progress further, usually some assumptions are made about the distribution from which we draw x. Namely, that F(x|eH) first-order stochastically dominates (FOSD)10 F(x|eL), or the even weaker property that the ratio $\frac{f(x|e_L)}{f(x|e_H)}$, which is known as the likelihood ratio ℒ(x), satisfies the monotone likelihood ratio property (MLRP).11 Using either assumption, in [[Asymmetric Information#Appendix C|Appendix C]] it is shown that both constraints must bind (strictly positive multipliers). This implies the following must be true for the optimal compensation (where w̃ is some fixed wage payment):
w(x) > w̃ if ℒ < 1
and
w(x) < w̃ if ℒ > 1

It is important to note that if we only have FOSD then, while the above condition holds, the wage is not necessarily monotonically increasing. For example, we could have a lower wage payment when x = 5 than when x = 4 (if L(x) < 1 in both cases). For the wage to be monotonically increasing in output we need MLRP to hold.

Adverse selection

Akerlof (1970) Market for lemons

Early pioneering research into asymmetric information included [[Akerlof (1970)]] and is still used as the example to illustrate the problem of adverse selection.

Suppose a seller values his car at v U[0, 1] and the buyer values it at γv where γ ≥ 1. The buyer will name a price and the seller can choose to accept, if v ≤ b, or reject, if v > b. The buyer’s expected payoff is:
$$\mathbb{P}(v \leq b)\Big( \mathbb{E}[\gamma v | v \leq b] - b \Big) = b^2 (\frac{\gamma}{2} - 1)$$
Therefore, if γ > 2 the buyer will offer 1. However, if 1 ≤ γ < 2 the optimal bid is b* = 0. Trade is efficient in this range but it still breaks down!

Signaling and screening are two primary solutions to adverse selection. In signaling, the informed party moves first. For example, the car seller offers a warranty to signal the car is of high quality. In screening, the uninformed party moves first. For example, an insurance provider offers separate insurance for high and low health individuals, and ideally each type only finds one contract satisfactory. ### Signaling #### Spence (1973, 1974) Education as a signal Spence’s (1973 and 1974) education model is a pioneering example of signaling. The setting involves a high ability θH and low ability θL type agents who are choosing how much education to acquire. The firms are competitive and pay a wage equal to the expected productivity w = 𝔼[θ]. Education has no other value other than a signal in this setting, and it is costly (especially for the low ability types) to acquire (c(θ, e)).12 For any given level of education a firm places a probability that they are the high type μ(e). With adverse selection and no signaling, all agents would recieve w* = 𝔼[θ].

Complete information about agent’s type

As previously, we consider the complete information benchmark. Here it is quite straightforward: the firm will offer the high wage to the high ability type and low wage to the low ability type. Neither agent will invest in any education since it is costly and will not change their wage.

Now, in adverse selection problems we usually have two types of solution: - Separating equilibria: where different types of agent make different choices (here this is different levels of education); and - Pooling equilibria: where agents of different types make the same choice. ##### Separating equilibrium (high agent’s invest in education) Suppose the the firm offers a high wage to workers who invest in a certain level of education e*. They could distinguish the low types, who would find it more costly to invest in that level of education than the higher wage would justify, from the high ability types, who can obtain the education at a lower cost. First note two things: 1. In equilibrium, the high type will receive w*(e*(θH)) = θH and the low type will receive w*(e*(θL)) = θL. Why? Well, if they are separating, the firm assigns probability 1 to the belief about the type of the agent exerting effort e = e*(θH) (that is, μ(eH) = 1) and probability 1 to the belief about the type of agent exerting effort e = e*(θL) (that is, μ(eL) = 0). 2. e*(θL) = 0. Why? If not, then they would choose a positive amount of education (ê) but still receive w(ê) = θL. They could choose e = 0 and be strictly better off.

Now, for this equilibrium to hold we must ensure that the high type does not want to mimic the low type and vice versa (incentive compatibility (IC) constraints). That is, we want the high type to prefer choose an education level e*(θH) (this is ICH) and the low type to prefer to choose e*(θL) = 0 (this is ICH):13
θH − c(e*(θH), θH) ≥ θL

θL ≥ θH − c(e*(θL),
This implies we can have a separating equilibrium when:
c(e*(θH), θH) ≤ θH + θL ≤ c(e*(θH), θL)

Screening

Rothschild and Stiglitz (1976) Insurance markets

This model is typically summarized by the following graph (Picard, 2014): [[Rothschild-Stiglitz2.png]]

The graph: - The horizontal axis represents wealth in the non-accident state (w1) of the world and vertical axis in the accident state of the world (w2). - The endowment point E is the pair of outcomes when the agent has no insurance (a wealth in the non-accident state of the world wN and the accident state wA).14 E.g. premium on an insurance (for example, point C) is the difference on the x-axis between point C and point E. 15 - The 45 degree line represents full insurance.

The agents: - In this model there are two types of agents: high-risk (type 1 on the graph) and low-risk (type 2 on the graph). The high-risk is much more likely (with probability pH) to be in the accident state of the world than the low-risk type (with probability pL). - The agents are risk-averse and would like to insure themselves against the bad state of the world:
(1 − p)u(w1 − α1) + p u(w2 + α2)
- where α1 is the cost of the insurance and α2 is the payout in the accident state of the world.

The insurer: - We typically assume that the insurance market has free entry; if there is a profitable insurance contract that can be offered that the agents prefer more, then a company will offer it and take all market share (thus, insurer profits are driven to zero). That is:
(1 − p)α1 − p α2 = 0

Complete information about agent’s riskiness

In this scenario, the insurance company can observe which type of agent is the high-risk and low-risk type and offer contracts to both accordingly. We can set up the insurer’s problem as:
maxα1, α2 (1 − p) α1 + p α2

s.t.(1 − p) u(wN − α1) + p u(wA + α2) ≥ (1 − p) u(wN) + p u(wA)
Taking first-order conditions we obtain:
$$\frac{u'(w_N - \alpha_1)}{u'(w_A + \alpha_2)} = 1 \implies w_N - \alpha_1 = w_A + \alpha_2$$
That is, each agent receives full insurance. We can solve for the contract (the exact α1 and α2) by plugging this into the zero profit condition.16 That is, for each agent:
(α1, α2)H = (pH(wN − wA), (1 − pH)(wN − wA))
and
(α1, α2)L = (pL(wN − wA), (1 − pL)(wN − wA))

Incomplete Information about agent’s riskiness

When the insurer can’t observe the riskiness of the agent then the complete information contracts would not work. The high risk agents would pretend to be low risk agents and take this contract, which would result in negative profit for the insurer. We consider the two types of equilibrium contract introduced in the signaling section: pooling and separating.

Pooling equilibrium (everyone receives same insurance contract)

In this setting, a pooling equilibrium does not exist. This is easy to see graphically. In the figure at the start of the model, there in an isoprofit curve that is the weighted average of profit from the mix of high/low risk types $\overline{F}$. If an insurer introduced a pooled contract, such as C, other insurers could enter and offer contracts such as C′, which would be accepted by the low risk types but not the high risk types. This is known as ‘cream-skimming’. This would leave the “pooled” contact with nothing but high risk agents, rendering it unprofitable.

Separating equilibrium (a different contract for each type)

We could set up the insurer’s problem with IC and IR constraints, however, I will just argue graphically. We know the high-risk type wants to mimic the low-risk type, thus our contract will have ICH binding. This means the low-risk contract must be no better for the high-risk agent, than the offer he is accepting- thus the low-risk agent is offered C** (in the figure). Further, note that the high-risk type will have full risk sharing (be on the 45 degree line). Firstly, they have to be on the F1 line because of free entry, and secondly, if the high-risk type was not on the 45 degree line, a competitor could design a contract that is on the same profit curve but that the agent will prefer. [[rothschild stiglitz separ.png]] Source: Picard, 2014 - annotated with red line.

However, it is important to note that a separating equilibrium may not exist. Suppose there are sufficient numbers of low risk agents such that the pooled profit line $\overline{F}$ has points that are above the low-risk agents indifference curve (e.g. the red line in the figure), then an insurer could come along and offer a pooled contract on that line and all of the market share. However, since we already know that a pooled equilibrium does not exist, this will imply that no equilibrium exists in this scenario. There will always be a better contract.

Monopolistic screening

While monopolistic screening is an adverse selection problem, the techniques used are very similar to the principal agent problem in the [[Asymmetric Information#Moral hazard hidden action|Moral hazard]] section. Applications of the monopolistic screening framework include: Mirrlees’ taxation, second price discrimination, insurance, and regulating natural monopolies.

The monopolistic screening model discussed here will be a firm trying to discriminate their pricing to agents that have high (θH) and low (θL) taste for the product. The agents will have quasi-linear utility (linear in money): $v(x,\theta) - t=2 \theta \sqrt{x} -t$, where x is units of the good and t is the payment (assume the buyer’s outside option in 0).17 The seller produces the goods at unit cost c. If the seller could observe the type, he could simply equalize marginal cost and marginal benefit to give the first best allocations:18
$$x^{FB}(\theta)=\begin{cases} \displaystyle (x_H/c)^2 \quad \text{if} \theta=\theta_H \\ \displaystyle (x_L/c)^2 \quad \text{if} \theta=\theta_L \end{cases}$$

However, as it should be clear by now, the high type would prefer to mimic the low type to pay less. The seller’s problem becomes:19
max{(xL, tL), (xH, tH)}∑θπ(θ)[t(θ) − c x(θ)]

s.t. v(xL, θL) − tL ≥ v(0, θL) − 0

v(xH, θH) − tH ≥ v(0, θH) − 0

v(xL, θL) − tL ≥ v(xH, θL) − tH

v(xH, θH) − tH ≥ v(xL, θH) − tL

The first two are individual rationality constraints (IRθL and IRθH), where agents would rather accept the contract intended for them than their outside option. The latter two are incentive compatibility constraints (ICθLθH and IRθHθL), where in the first, the low type would prefer to accept his contract than to mimic the high type, and vice versa for the second.

Notice that we can rewrite the IC constraints as:
v(xH, θH) − v(xL, θH) ≥ tH − tL ≥ v(xH, θL) − v(xL, θL)

One of these equalities must be strict or the seller could charge more. ICθHθL will be the one to bind. We can reason this since the low type has no incentive to mimic the high type, but the high type will want to mimic the low type. Combining ICθHθL and IRθL implies that IRθH will be slack (the high type will earn information rents), and IRθL will be binding:
v(xH, θH) − tH − v(0, θH) (by IRθH) = v(xL, θH) − v(0, θH) − tL (by ICθHθL)

 ≥ v(xL, θL) − v(0, θL) − tL (since vxθ > 0) = 0 (since seller could profit if IRθL slack)

The problem is then reduced to two constraints (since the slack conditions can be ignored)20 and one can easily take first-order conditions to find the optimal (second-best) allocations and transfers. It should be noted that the high type achieves the first-best quantity, but the low type doesn’t (low type quantity is distorted downward).
