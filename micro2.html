    ii. Notation

    Vectors are lower case and bold (e.g. \(\mathbf{\phi}(\mathbf{p},m)\)) and sets are capitalized (e.g. \(B\)). Derivatives are row vectors (e.g. \(\mathbf{D_m}\mathbf{h}(\mathbf{p},c)\)) and gradients are column vectors (e.g. \(\nabla_m v(\mathbf{p},m)\)). We are currently only dealing with one consumer and he has \(n\) goods to choose from. Highlighting in teal represents important identities/concepts, light green represents equations that link identities/concepts and the main results/theorems are in orange. \(\mathbf{x}\) and \(\boldsymbol{\phi}\) are mostly interchangeable, I use the former describing a bundle and the latter describes demand for a bundle (this follows John Nachbar's preference to denote two different concepts with different symbols).

    Preferences and Utility

    We can represent preferences satisfying weak behavioural assumptions by a continuous utility function. This function is not unique and is invariant to positive monotonic transformations. Often there is no significance is attached to the numbers from a utility function, only to the order (ordinal). However, if we use a specific utility form, such as quasi-linear form, we can make utility cardinal (i.e. it measures the intensity of preference). The utility function inherits some properties from preferences and to use calculus tools we assume some additional mathematical properties of utility.

    Utility Maximization

    A consumer maximizes this utility subject to a budget constraint. The solution to this utility maximization problem (UMP) gives a vector \(\mathbf{x}(\mathbf{p},m)=(x_1,x_2,\ldots,x_n)'\) outlining how much of each good we would have at and income level \(m\) and prices \(\mathbf{p}=(p_1,p_2,\ldots,p_n)'\). This solution is Marshallian demand \(\mathbf{\phi}(\mathbf{p},m)\). Based on the assumptions of the utility function we can derive some mathematical properties of \(\mathbf{\phi}(\mathbf{p},m)\).

    We can already make some useful predictions about demand just from the properties of \(\mathbf{\phi}(\mathbf{p},m)\). Firstly, we can conclude that the price and wealth derivatives of demand for any good, when weighted by these prices and wealth, sum to zero (\(D_p\mathbf{\phi}(\mathbf{p},m) \cdot \mathbf{p} + D_m\mathbf{\phi}(\mathbf{p},m) \cdot m=0\)). Alternatively, the elasticities of prices and wealth sum to zero (equal percentage change in all prices and wealth leads to no change in demand). Additionally, we can conclude two additional facts (to be precise, the differential versions of these facts) that are known as Cournot and Engel aggregation, respectively: total expenditure cannot change in response to a change in prices and that total expenditure must change by an amount equal to any wealth change.

    Once we know how much we'll have at any given m and \(\mathbf{p}\), we know what our utility level is, by plugging demand back into the utility function: \(v(\mathbf{p},m) = u(\mathbf{\phi}(\mathbf{p},m))\). This is indirect utility. We can see it inherits some nice mathematical properties also, including Roy's identity which lets us go the other way (get \(\mathbf{\phi}(\mathbf{p},m)\) from \(v(\mathbf{p},m)\)): $$\mathbf{\phi}(\mathbf{p},m) = - \frac{1}{D_mv(\mathbf{p},m)} \nabla v(\mathbf{p},m)$$

    The Dual: Expenditure Minimization

    We can also tackle everything from a slightly different perspective. Instead of maximizing utility subject to a budget, let's minimize expenditure: \begin{equation} \min_{\mathbf{x}} \mathbf{p \cdot x} \quad \text{such that} \quad u(\mathbf{x})\geq c \end{equation} The value of the expenditure minimization problem (EMP) is called the expenditure function (\(e(\mathbf{p},c)\)) and is the minimum expenditure level that will give us at least a certain amount of utility, c. Thus, \(\mathbf{p}\cdot \mathbf{x^*}\), where \(\mathbf{x^*}\), solves the EMP; and the bundle \(\mathbf{x^*}\) that minimizes expenditure is called Hicksian demand (\(e(\mathbf{p},c) = \mathbf{p} \cdot \mathbf{h}(\mathbf{p},c)\)) and it is the analogue of \(\mathbf{\phi}(\mathbf{p},m)\). We can similarly derive some mathematical properties from preferences, as well as properties about it's derivative with respect to price \(\mathbf{D_p}\mathbf{h}(\mathbf{p},c)\). What's the point of doing this dual problem - and how does it relate to the UMP? It's usefulness will become evident in a bit, but I'll just say that it's quite convenient to work with the expenditure function. We can readily say some things about it's mathematical properties .}. A key property which allows us to get (Hicksian) demand is simply to differentiate \(e(\mathbf{p},c)\). This is known as Shephard's lemma: \begin{equation*} \mathbf{h}(\mathbf{p},c) = \nabla e(\mathbf{p},c) \end{equation*}

    The idea behind this lemma is that the effect of a price change on expenditure is the result of a change in prices for a fixed demand and the result of a change in demand for fixed prices (since we're at an expenditure minimizing bundle, the latter is zero). Finally, in addition to properties about Hicksian demand, we can also derive some important properties about how Hicksian demand changes with price (\(\mathbf{D_p}\mathbf{h}(\mathbf{p},c)\))..}

    The Compensated Law of Demand

    The Hicksian demand leads us to the very major use of the theory of demand we're building: the Compensated Law of Demand, which says (Hicksian) demand and prices move in opposite directions for price changes that are accompanied by a Hicksian wealth compensation (the Hicksian wealth compensation is the amount of wealth you need to compensate a consumer to keep the same utility level, \(c\), after a price change). An immediate implication is that Hicksian demands have non-positive own-price effects (for example, if the price of potatoes rises you might buy more potatoes, however, if I gave you money to compensate you for your lower utility, then you will buy the same or less potatoes than before).     That is, prices and demand move in opposite directions if wealth is adjusted after the price change so as to make the initial consumption bundle just as affordable at new prices. This holds for the Hicksian demand, but not necessarily for Marshallian demand. Indeed, it shows that Hicksian demand only picks up the substitution effect of an own-rice change, while the Marshallian demand picks up the total effect of an own-price effect (it includes the income effect). This will shortly be expanded upon further in the Slutsky equation section.

    This is nice, but there's a problem. We don't actually observe Hicksian demand since we can't observe someone's level of utility. To make this law more useful we need to be able to relate the concepts we've found\ldots

    Relating Things Together

    \ldots Luckily, the four green concepts listed above relate to each other closely and nicely. We already have identities to get from Marshallian demand to indirect utility and from the expenditure function to Hicksian Demand.

    It is clear that at the optimal bundle the Hicksian demand and Marshallian demand will be equal for the appropriate values of \(w\) and \(c\). That is, if \(\mathbf{x^*}\) is optimal in the UMP for wealth \(w\), it is optimal in the EMP when \(c=u(\mathbf{x^*})\). Similarly, if \(\mathbf{x^*}\) is optimal in the EMP for a given utility \(c\), then it is optimal in the UMP when wealth is \(p\cdot \mathbf{x^*}\). We can restate this to yield the following relationship between Hicksian and Marshallian demands: \begin{align*} \mathbf{h}(\mathbf{p},c) &= \mathbf{\phi(\mathbf{p},e(\mathbf{p},c))} \\ \mathbf{\phi}(\mathbf{p},m) &= \mathbf{h(\mathbf{p},v(\mathbf{p},w))} \end{align*}

    Slutsky Decomposition

    The Compensated Law of Demand gave us a nice way to see the effect of a change in prices on the change in Hicksian demand (the substitution effect), however, we can do better. Since we know that changes in Marshallian demand pick up the total effect and the changes in Hicksian demand pick up the substitution effect, we can decompose the effect of a price change on demand into a part caused by price changes in demand by holding utility constant (substitution effects) and those caused by changes in real income (income effects) - this is the Slutsky decomposition.

    The Slutsky equation (sometimes called the Slutsky decomposition or Slutsky matrix) is arrived at by differentiating Hicksian demand: $$\mathbf{D_p}\mathbf{\phi}(\mathbf{p},m) = \mathbf{D_p}\mathbf{h}(\mathbf{p},c) - \mathbf{D_m}\mathbf{\phi}(\mathbf{p},m) \mathbf{x}$$ The Slutsky equation embodies essentially all of our information about the behavior of demand, and is sometimes called the `Fundamental Equation of Demand Theory'!

    We know a fair bit about \(\mathbf{D_p \mathbf{h}(\mathbf{p},c)}\) from Section X, which is known as the Slutsky substitution matrix. However, we must know more about preferences (e.g. homotheticity) to have a stronger Law of Demand, or say more about what will happen to demand given a price change.

    Choice-based Approach to Demand Theory: The Weak Axiom

    Okay, so we've built up a theory of demand which makes assumptions about preferences and deduces the properties demand will have. However, Samuelson (1947) suggested an alternative approach, under which demand can be derived by simple and sensible assumptions on observable choices, rather than unobservable preferences. It can be shown that, under certain "reasonable" restrictions on an individual's choice behavior, the demand derived by this approach will be equivalent to the one from the utility maximization framework.

    An important restriction on behavior is that of the Weak Axiom of Revealed Preference (WARP), which imposes that observed choices will display a certain amount of consistency. The weak axiom comes from decision theory and at it's most simplified is, when one choice is selected over another, it is revealed to be preferred. If for some \(B\in \mathcal{B}\) with \(x,y \in B\) we have \(x\in C(B)\), then for any \(B' \in \mathcal{B}\) with \(x,y \in B'\) and \(y \in C(B')\), we must also have \(x \in C(B')\).} However, we can more simply state the Weak Axiom in terms of a revealed preference relation: \(x\) RW \(y\) \(\iff\) there is some \(B \in \mathcal{B}\) such that \(x,y \in B\) and \(x \in C(B)\).} `If \(x\) is revealed at least as good as \(y\), then \(y\) cannot be revealed preferred to \(x\).'

    To analyze the implications of the Weak Axiom on demand, we typically add some assumptions on demand \(\mathbf{\phi}(\mathbf{p},m)\) is:

    • Homogeneous of degree zero (no money illusion). Note: this is what allows us to normalize \(p_1=1\) (a numéraire).
    • Satisfies Walras' law (\(\mathbf{p}\cdot \mathbf{\phi}(\mathbf{p},m) =m\)).
    The implications of these assumptions was previously discussed.} and rewrite the Weak Axiom in terms of this demand. It can be shown (Appendix) that the Weak Axiom is equivalent to the Compensated Law of Demand. Further, the assumptions and the WARP imply that the Slutsky matrix is negative semidefinite.

    The above results imply that the WARP is necessary for the demand theory of previous sections. Indeed, it is easy to show that if you have rational preferences they will generate a choice structure that will satisfy the weak axiom. If \(x RW y\) for \(B\) (\(\implies x W y\)), and \(y\in C(B')\) for some \(B'\) (which contains \(x,y\)), then \(y W z\). Thus, by transitivity \(x W z\) and so \(x \in C(B')\) also, as the WA requires.} The preferences that generate this choice structure do not need to be unique. However, an issue arises in the other direction, in showing that preferences rationalize a choice structure (some preferences would result in the same choices). It is easy to generate an example where choice satisfies the Weak Axiom and the preferences that rationalize the choices are not transitive. \(\mathcal{B}=\{ \{ x, y \}, \{ x, z\}, \{ y, z\} \}\) and \(C(\{ x, y \})=\{ x\}, C(\{ y, z\})=\{ y\}, C(\{ x, z\})=\{ z\}\).} Therefore, the theories are not equivalent. The choice-based approach yields a weaker theory than the one derived from rational preferences (since the preference-based approach required a symmetric Slustky matrix, which is not required here). Indeed, the gap between WA being necessary and sufficient is the gap between necessary and sufficient conditions of the maximization problem. That is, if we have twice differentiability of demand, then Young's theorem implies symmetry of the Slutsky matrix and the two theories are equivalent. Further, there is a deep connection between transitivity and symmetry of the Slutsky matrix, which is not explored here.}

    If \(\mathcal{B}\) includes all subsets of \(X\) that have 2 or 3 elements then we will not face the issue of a failure of transitivity. Since this guarantees transitivity. See MWG 1.D for proof.} However, this is too special of a case to guarantee the equivalence of the choice and preference approaches in consumer demand (where we only define choice on a subset of budget sets). However, Houthakker (1950) found a slightly stronger restriction on preferences than the weak axiom that is necessary and sufficient to ensure it will result in the same demand behaviour as a preference-based approach. It is known as the Strong Axiom of Revealed Preference (SARP), and it is effectively a version of the weak axiom that rules out intransitivity.

    Reverse Direction: Integrability

    To recap: from preference's we have built up utility and demand functions. However, it should be noted that under certain conditions we can go the other way. From observed demand, or expenditure, we can generate preferences that rationalize \(\mathbf{\phi}(\mathbf{p},m)\). This is known as Integrability. There are theoretical and practical reasons for why we would want to do this. Firstly, it confirms the findings we have for demand are not only necessary but they are all the consequences. These conditions are budget balancedness, negative semi-definiteness and symmetry of the Slutsky substitution matrix.} That is, if you find an equation satisfying all these conditions, then this is a demand function. Practically, it tells us how we can construct utility functions from the data. It is an important tool in telling us what we need to complete demand functions just by observing consumer's choices.

    Regarding the first point, that there are no other restrictions that we need to impose on a preference-based demand theory, the result goes back to Antonelli (1886), who showed that with the three essential conditions there must exist a utility function that generates a given vector-valued function of income and prices. A useful implication of this relates to the choice-based theory of demand. As we have seen, the weak axiom is not sufficient to rationalize preferences. However, we can see that, in addition to homogeneity and Walras' law, adding symmetry of the substitution matrix is all that we need to guarantee we can rationalize preferences.

    Practically, we can recover preferences by, first, recovering an expenditure function \(e(\mathbf{p},c)\) from observed demand \(\mathbf{\phi}(\mathbf{p},m)\), and secondly, recovering preferences from the expenditure function. The latter is rather difficult to do and involves solving the following partial differential equation: \begin{equation*} \frac{\partial e(p_1)}{\partial p_1} = \boldsymbol{\phi}(p_1) \end{equation*} The second step is best understood graphically (MWG 3.H.1). However, in practice there is only finite data, which means there will be many utility functions that fit the data. Arifat (1967) showed that utility can be taken to be continuous and concave.
